Datasets

We utilize a variety of datasets to train our mmWave reconstruction network:

  • OmniObject3D: A large multi-view 3D dataset comprised of 6,000 scans of real objects in 190 categories.
  • Toys4K-3D: A dataset consisting of 4,179 3D objects in 105 categories that capture objects encountered by infants and children.
  • Thingiverse (Objaverse): A subset of around 3.5 million objects from the Thingiverse platform. Most contain untextured meshes, which is useful for learning shape priors.
To evaluate the performance of our model, we use real-world mmWave measurements of 61 objects from the YCB dataset. These objects have diverse properties, both material and geometric, such as wooden v.s. metal and flat v.s. curved. To further test real-world application, both line-of-sight and fully-obstructed mmWave data of each object are used.

Training

Existing reconstruction models are often trained on vision-like partials which come from visible-light sensors such as cameras or LiDAR. However, these sensors have diffuse reflections and wide, uniform coverage, contrary to the specular, anisotropic, and noisy data received from mmWaves. Thus, in order to shift domains and train our mmWave reconstruction network, we must first encode the physical characteristics of mmWaves into our training architecture.

First, we construct a training dataset entirely from synthetic point clouds derived from public 3D shape datasets. To embed mmWave specularity directly into these synthetic samples, we retain only the points that would (1) produce a specular return and (2) lie on the surface facing the radar. The first condition models the fact that mmWave reflections follow a specular law: a point is visible only if the incoming ray and reflected ray form equal angles with the surface normal in their shared plane. The second condition ensures the training partials only include geometry that physically “faces” the radar, since mmWave sensors rarely receive energy from back-facing or oblique surfaces. In other words, we create the set of partial observations $O$ such that \begin{align*} O &= \left\{ s_i \in F \,\middle|\, \theta_P(s_i) < \tau ,\; V(s_i) = 1 \right\}\\\\ \theta_P(s_i)&=\min_{r\in P}|\arccos(n_i\cdot u_{r,i})|,\, u_{r,i}=\frac{p_r-s_i}{||p_r-s_i||} \end{align*}

where $s_i$ is a point in the full 3D point cloud $F$ from the public datasets. $\theta_P(s_i)$ is the smallest angular mismatch between $s_i$ and any sensor position in $P$, the set of of possible radar positions, where $n_i$ is the surface normal at $s_i$ and $u_{r,i}$ is the unit vector pointing from $s_i$ to $p_r$. We choose the parameter $\tau$ to bound $\theta_P(s_i)$. $V(s_i)$ is 1 when $s_i$ lies on the radar facing surface and 0 otherwise.

Next, we further refine the specular partials by keeping only those points that exhibit anisotropic behavior, which captures the fact that mmWave measurements vary with the angle of incidence and the corresponding reflection strength of each surface. To model this behavior, we construct a reflection-dependent visibility filter that selects surface points according to physical constraints on incidence angle and material reflectivity. In other words, we produce the set $O'$ such that \begin{align*} O'=\{s_i\in O\;|\;\theta_H&(n_i)<\tau_H,\;\theta_V(n_i)<\tau_V\}\\\\ \theta_H=|\arccos&(n_i\cdot\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0\end{bmatrix})|\\\\ \theta_V=|\arccos&(n_i\cdot\begin{bmatrix}0&1&0\end{bmatrix})| \end{align*}

where $\theta_H$ and $\theta_V$ are the angles of the horizontal and vertical specular returns from the point $s_i$. $\tau_H$ and $\tau_V$ are parameters that we choose to simulate different material properties.

Finally, we inject Gaussian noise into the synthetic partial point clouds $O'$ to mimic the substantial noise present in real mmWave measurements. To ensure the model learns both to denoise and complete these inputs, we replace the concatenation-based loss that PoinTr uses with the bi-directional Chamfer Distance between the model's predicted point cloud $\hat{F}$ and the ground-truth $F$. $$ \mathcal{L}=\frac{1}{|\hat{F}|}\sum_{s_i\in \hat{F}}\min_{g\in F}||s_i-g||+\frac{1}{|F|}\sum_{g\in F}\min_{s_i\in \hat{F}}||g-s_i|| $$ Intuitively, the bi-directional Chamfer Distance measures how far the predicted shape is from the true shape in both directions: every predicted point must lie close to some ground-truth point and every ground-truth point must be close to some predicted point. This encourages the model to reinterpret the noisy inputs rather than preserve them, allowing it to output a fully denoised and complete point cloud $\hat{F}$.
